Ocean Animation

This was a final project for 6.837: Computer Graphics. Based on the paper "Interactive Animation of Ocean Waves" (Hinsinger et. al, 2002), I implemented a real-time deep ocean surface animation by first projecting screen points to the flat plane, then using a procedural model for trochoid-based wave movement, and finally reprojecting screen points when the camera position changes. Different camera angles, wave parameters, lighting parameters, and colors were tested for a more realistic rendering.

Texture Synthesis and Image Inpainting

I compared the motivation and results of texture synthesis and image inpainting for my 6.865 Computational Photography final project. For texture synthesis and hole-filling, I coded the method in "Texture Synthesis by Non-Parametric Sampling" (Efros and Leung, 1999) over images of both stochastic and tiled patterns, by implementing a per-pixel window sampling algorithm Gaussian blur and a normalized sum of squared differences. For image inpainting, I implemented the iterative method in "Image Inpainting" (Bertalmio, Sapiro, Caselles and Ballester, 2000), which aims to connect isophite lines at region boundaries using the structure tensor calculated from the luminance gradients and Poisson-based gradient descent, and then estimates coloration using anisotropic diffusion.

Few-shot learning for rare cardiovascular disease detection

This was a group project for the 6.871: Machine Learning in Healthcare class. We proposed a metalearning approach, specifically a few-shot learning approach, to handle classification of rare conditions as well as generalization to unseen rare conditions. We pretrained a CNN architecture on healthy patients and some common conditions, and transfer learn this model onto few shot training tasks for rare disease detection. Within the project, my tasks included data processing to turn 10,000 patient ECG readings to input and label tensors, writing parts of the twin network model, implementing some of the training and dataloader processes, and helping with the hyperparameter and validation set optimization, all using Pytorch. In our final paper, I wrote on cohort/feature selection; AUC, hyperparameter, loss, and similarity results; and clinical relevance. Our results achieved 0.35 in top 5 matching, and 32/45 patients with at least one top 2 matching out of 2 conditions.

COVID Protease Inhibition via Random Matrix Theory

This was a group project for the 18.065: Matrix Methods in Data Analysis, Signal Processing, and Machine Learning class. We propose a linear algrebra-based random matrix theory method to analyze coronavirus protease inhibition data to understand potential similarities between compounds that inhibit protease function, and compare this to experimental techniques such as RNNs. My tasks included implementing methods described in "Predicting protein–ligand affinity with a random matrix framework" (Lee, Brenner, Colwell, 2016) using numpy to find statistically significant eigenvectors using the MP distribution of protein Morgan fingerprints’ correlation matrix eigenvalues and build a subspace for projection of evaluation set proteins for inhibitory activity identification.
